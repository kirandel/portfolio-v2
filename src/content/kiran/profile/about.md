### **Early Life & Upbringing**

Kiran grew up in Southeast Asia, with a background shaped by movement, resilience, and exposure to very different cultures from an early age. His parents met in a refugee camp, and he spent the first few years of his life along the border of Cambodia and Thailand.

Growing up across countries and cultures had a lasting impact on how he sees the world. It made him highly adaptable, comfortable in unfamiliar environments, and naturally curious about how people, systems, and societies work.

As a kid, he was constantly taking things apart and trying to put them back together. That instinct—to deconstruct, understand, and rebuild—became a defining thread in how he approaches problems today. At its core, it’s the same instinct that drives how he builds products: understand how something works, identify what’s broken or missing, and make it better.

This combination of global exposure and builder instinct shaped two core traits early on:

* A strong curiosity about how systems (technical and human) work  
* An ability to connect with and build for a wide range of people

### **Early Interest in Technology**

Kiran’s interest in technology started early and very concretely.

One of his first vivid memories was being around 10 years old in Laos, using a family computer on a slow dial-up connection, when his mom showed him how Google worked. The idea that you could type anything into a box and instantly access the world’s information was mind-blowing.

From that point on, he was hooked.

He spent his time:

* Tinkering with computers and software  
* Building early websites and customizing things like MySpace pages  
* Experimenting with light “hacking” (more curiosity-driven than anything malicious)  
* Exploring design and creative tools  
* Mixing music and learning how digital systems could be used creatively

What stands out in hindsight is that it wasn’t just usage—it was always *interaction \+ modification*. He wasn’t satisfied just using products; he wanted to understand how they worked and how to shape them.

### **First Real Exposure to Product Building**

Kiran’s first true exposure to product thinking came from building his own business: **truenorthposters.com**.

The idea originated while taking a GIS (Geographic Information Systems) course at Berkeley. He noticed that the maps he was generating—when styled correctly—looked visually compelling, almost like art.

He had a simple insight:

People who travel might want beautifully designed maps of meaningful places on their walls.

Instead of leaving it as an idea, he decided to build it.

Over the course of \~6 months, he:

* Designed and built the website  
* Created an interactive map generation system  
* Sourced and managed suppliers  
* Built a marketing strategy  
* Solved logistics and fulfillment  
* Launched the product end-to-end

The business became profitable within 3 months of launch.

This experience was foundational because it compressed the full product lifecycle into a single project:

* Idea → validation → build → launch → growth

More importantly, it taught him that he didn’t need permission to build something real. That mindset—high agency, end-to-end ownership, and bias toward execution—became central to how he operates as a product manager.

### **Transition from Marketing → Product Management**

Kiran started his career at Turo in a marketing role, where he spent his first two years focused on growth, acquisition, and performance optimization.

While he found marketing intellectually interesting—especially around metrics, experimentation, and optimization—he quickly realized something important:

He didn’t just want to grow products. He wanted to *build them*.

The inflection point came when an internal tool at Turo—a calculator that helped hosts understand which vehicles would generate the best ROI—was abandoned due to lack of engineering bandwidth.

Instead of letting it die, he took initiative.

He went to his manager and asked if he could rebuild the product himself on evenings and weekends. With support, he started teaching himself how to design and build the tool from scratch.

That experience became a turning point.

Through rebuilding the calculator, he discovered that he loved:

* Talking directly to users (hosts) to understand their needs  
* Translating those needs into product features  
* Designing and iterating on the experience  
* Measuring performance and improving the product over time  
* Owning something end-to-end

It wasn’t just one part of the process—it was the *entire loop* that energized him.

That project became his first real exposure to product management in practice. Over the next couple of years, he leaned further into product work, gradually transitioning from marketing into a full-time PM role.

At a high level, the transition wasn’t accidental—it was driven by a clear realization:

Building products sat at the intersection of everything he enjoyed: users, systems, design, data, and execution.

### **Major Career Turning Point: Building a New Line of Business (0 → 1\)**

One of the most defining moments in Kiran’s career came when he was given the opportunity to lead a small, highly autonomous team to explore a new product concept.

The insight was simple but powerful:

There were many vehicle owners who wanted to earn passive income on Turo but didn’t have the time or operational capability to manage hosting. At the same time, there were professional hosts who *did* have operational capacity but were constrained by access to vehicles.

The opportunity:

Create a system that matches vehicle owners with professional hosts—effectively unlocking new supply and enabling a new business model.

Despite the potential, there was no engineering or design resourcing available.

Kiran pitched the idea directly to leadership, including the CEO, and was given the opportunity to run with it.

He was assigned a small team of five operators, and together they were tasked with building the product from scratch—with no dedicated engineering or design support.

This meant:

* Designing the product experience  
* Building the operational workflows  
* Creating the matching logic (including personally coding the initial algorithm)  
* Running pilots and experiments  
* Validating product-market fit

They launched an initial pilot in two cities, and the results were strong. The product demonstrated clear value for both sides of the marketplace and showed early signs of scalable product-market fit.

That initiative has since evolved into a significant new business line for Turo, on track to generate tens of millions in annual volume.

More importantly, this experience was a true inflection point for Kiran:

* It validated his ability to build 0 → 1 products under real constraints  
* It reinforced his bias toward action and ownership  
* It gave him early experience leading a team and setting product direction

### **Key Challenge & Lesson: Talk to Users Earlier**

One of the most formative lessons in Kiran’s early product experience came from building the initial version of the host ROI calculator.

At first, he focused heavily on building a system that leveraged as much *live, real-time data* as possible, assuming that more precision and more data would lead to a better product.

In reality, that wasn’t what users needed.

Hosts didn’t necessarily want hyper-dynamic, constantly changing inputs. What they actually needed was:

* Simplicity  
* Clarity  
* Reliable, directional guidance

In hindsight, the biggest mistake wasn’t technical—it was process:

He didn’t talk to users early enough.

If he had spent more time upfront speaking directly with hosts, he would have quickly understood that a simpler, more “evergreen” model would have been both easier to build and more useful.

That experience shaped a core principle he still uses today:

* Talk to users early  
* Validate assumptions quickly  
* Don’t over-engineer before understanding the real need
